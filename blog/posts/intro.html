Today I am wrapping up my second week as a student at Telegraph Academy. Telegraph Academy (TGA) is a bootcamp-style programming school that caters to people of color and people who are underrepresented in the tech industry. TGA opened earlier this year in partnership with Hack Reactor, and I am in the second cohort of students - TGA[1]. I recently made the tough decision to quit my job and put my life on hold to learn how to program for 11+ hours each day. Everyone said it would be difficult, and boy were they right.

Before classes even started, we had several weeks worth of precourse work to complete. Keep in mind that at this point, every student had already passed a technical interview to demonstrate their JavaScript understanding. The precourse work was riddled with new concepts and challenging javascript prompts. During this time, we had our first introduction to libraries (e.g., Underscore and jQuery) and to Recursion (more on that later).

Then classes started, and there has been a whirlwind of information flying at us ever since. Remember that game you used to play at birthday parties at the roller rink, where someone goes into a telephone booth-looking contraption, and tries to grab as many flying $1 bills as they can? It seems so simple when you're wathcing someone else do it. Then you give it a try, realize you're in way over your head, and you struggle to catch ANY of the $1 bills. Programming bootcamp is pretty much like that, but in the best possible way.

In the past two weeks, we have covered a plethora of information at lightning speed. We have had practice with:

  • Closures
  • Instantiation patterns
  • Prototype chains
  • Data structures
  • Algorithms
  • Subclassing patterns
  • The d3 library

We completed a new project for each of these topics, receiving a new project every two days. Talk about exhausting. However, this learning style really suits me - we are constantly throwing ourselves into challenging situations and figuring it out along the way. The past two days were perhaps the most intimidating, after being tasked with building a game that is based on the d3 library, starting from a blank javascript file. As overwhelming as that project was initially, my partner and I were able to break it down, struggle through it, and figure most of it out.

As I progress through this course, I will post updates here regarding specific javascript and programming topics. Of course, some non-programming topics might sneak their way in...
